Case Study: The "Pending Semester Fee" Phishing Scam

1. What is the Crime?  
This case focuses on phishing, specifically smishing, which is phishing through SMS. Phishing is a cyberattack where criminals pretend to be a legitimate organization to trick individuals into giving away sensitive information like login credentials, credit card numbers, or UPI PINs.

2. Who is Targeted?  

The Primary Targets: College and university students.  

Why they are targeted: Students often make online payments for tuition, exam fees, and hostel dues. They are also very active on their phones and may quickly click links when faced with urgent academic deadlines.

3. How it Happens (Step-by-Step Execution)  

Step 1: The Bait (Urgency and Fear): The attacker sends a mass SMS to students, disguising the sender ID to appear like the university administration. The message says, "URGENT: Your semester fee payment of ₹15,000 has failed. Your enrollment will be suspended by 5 PM today. Click here to complete payment immediately: [Malicious Link]"

Step 2: The Deception (Fake Gateway): A worried student clicks the link, leading them to a fake website. This site is designed to look exactly like the university's official payment portal or a typical UPI payment gateway.

Step 3: Data Harvesting (The Trap): The student, thinking they are resolving a real issue, enters their banking information or scans a fake QR code and provides their UPI PIN to process the payment.

Step 4: The Exploit (Financial Theft): Instead of paying the university, the credentials and authorization are sent directly to the cybercriminal. The attacker quickly drains the specified amount (or more) from the student's bank account.

4. The Consequences  

Direct Financial Loss: The student loses the money taken from their account, which is often hard or impossible to recover right away.  

Secondary Academic Impact: The student still owes the actual fee to the university, putting them under severe financial stress.  

Data Compromise: The student's phone number and banking information are now on a "sucker list" sold on the dark web, making them a target for future scams.  

Psychological Distress: Victims often feel significant stress, anxiety, and a loss of trust in digital communication.
